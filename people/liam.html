Research

My previous research suggests that signaling networks are extremely plastic. Throughout the eukaryotic lineage, we observe that similar signaling proteins control similar cell biological events. However, the precise architectures of signaling pathways vary substantially. I believe that this is because there is selective pressure to optimize the precise spatial and temporal qualities of signaling networks as organisms alter their size, shape and lifespan. For example, a single-celled organism competing in an abundance of resources will optimize to divide and increase biomass as quickly as possible, whereas a metazoan cell must divide in a specific geometry and with a precise timing to facilitate development.

The rapid rewiring that we observe in post-translational signaling systems seems to be enabled by the use of plastic, easy to evolve mechanisms of regulation such as breaking protein-protein interactions or the generation of modular interactions (e.g. SH2 or 14-3-3 proteins binding to phosphoepitopes). However, these inferences remain to be tested. The Lim lab is currently focused on these issues: here I hope to use synthetic biology approaches to try to define general evolutionary principles.
